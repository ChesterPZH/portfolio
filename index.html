Vision of Work

Exploring Spatially Augmented Human-Computer Interaction

"Yet almost all new products reduce interaction to pushing a labeled button They viewed this trend as a failure to account for people as sensual beings; a failure to focus on perceptual motor skills as a source of interaction inspiration, due to an overreliance on cognition as a singular theoretical stance in interaction design, and a failure to consider aesthetics as a critical component of interaction."

‚Äî Ways of Knowing in HCI

Conventional interfaces fall short for spatially grounded activities, while humans inherently engage with the world spatially. My research vision is to design spatially augmented Human-Computer interaction systems that embed digital content into the practice of real life, restoring interaction to its embodied roots in support of contextual activities.

I am particularly interested in constructing interfaces with situational intelligence using the 3D digital resources available in the contemporary spatial computing ecosystem. These include all representational and computational content that exists within such systems or can be captured by them: geometric and spatial reconstruction, motion and pose information, graphic rendering, animated assets, and environment states. Together, these resources provide a structured spatial substrate that an interface can leverage both to understand how a situation unfolds and to generate outputs that adapt to it.

rulAR: Spatial Measurement in Indoor Scenes via Intelligent Labeling and Immersive Visualization

Pan, Zhaohan

To appear in ACM ICHEC '25   

Intro

Existing augmented reality (AR) measuring tools enforce a monotonic measurement pattern that requires precise manual procedure. As spatial computing enters everyday practice, we present rulAR, an innovative AR spatial-scene measurement experiential prototype to surpass this stagnation. rulAR highlights low-manual to zero-manual task operation on an intelligent, scene-aware spatial interface: it intelligently labels measurement-critical points in the scene for selection, or automatically delivers comprehensive visualized dimensions immersively.


Development

rulAR is developed on Vision Pro (visionOS 2.0), with all designs completed using Apple's internal developer resources, including ARKit, RealityKit, and UIKit.
PlaneAnchor serves as the core of this work for obtaining the initial raw scene data, and we specifically utilized its high-fidelity mesh overlay data for rulAR.

A robust backend pipeline of geometric scene reconstruction and rendering is implemented. One essential part is a a two-step optimization algorithm to re-construct raw mesh boundaries into useful attributes for rulAR's interface. This includes a Rotating Calipers MinArea Search and a Quantile-Trimming Huber Search, detailed in the paper.


Interactivity

By providing critical points in the scene, user can use lightweight gaze-tap input to achieve select-measure process to query any interested dimension in the current environment. Measurement interaction can be also achieved purely through visual communication by directly rendering the plane-rectangle data.

rulAR presents a re-imagination of indoor digital measurement paradigm that is suitable for architectural design and engineering construction context.

mARker: Hybrid-Interfaced Spatial Sketching via iPad AR, Apple Pencil, and an Instantly Crafted Tracker

Pan, Zhaohan

In Proceedings of IEEE ISMAR '25 Adjunct    DOI   

Intro

Mobile AR promises wider access to spatial sketching, but existing designs either pack interactions into dense on-screen controls and constant device motion, or impose additional hardware barriers.
mARker is a proof-of-concept on the iPad + Apple Pencil Pro ecosystem, with a spatial & on-screen hybrid interface. It attains reliable stylus pose with a low-cost, self-fabricated cube and uses developer guideline-aligned Pencil gestures for writing-hand intuitive input, while AR session affords natural proxy-plane access and scene-aware placement.


Development

mARker's AR app is built under iPadOS 17.5 and supports two interfaces : a spatial interface for mid-air sketching via stylus tracking, and an on-screen interface for proxy plane sketching, entity transformation, along with auxiliary controls such as color selection.

The development of mARker benefits greatly from the new APIs introduced at WWDC 24 for Apple Pencil Pro. We implemented two sketching modes using these APIs: freehand and ruler. double-tap serves as a global toggle between these modes, while squeeze and barrel-roll are assigned to interface-specific sketching functions.


Interactivity

With mARker, user can directly sketch mid-air 3D content, or use traditional way to sketch projected 3D content and manipulate the stroke entities. Practical scenarios are introduced, including in-situ analysis diagram annotation on real object, and dimensioning an object by physically tracing it and evaluating the dimension.

Built on standardized tablet hardware and a hybrid stylus interface, mARker serves as a proof-of-concept ubiquitous spatial sketching tool that runs on commodity personal devices with a self-fabricatable tracking cube. These choices reduce operation overhead and hardware burden, broadening the reach and expressiveness of MAR spatial sketching.

karP: an experiential prototype for Kinesthetic Augmented Reality on mobile ‚Äî Playful, Portable, Producible

Pan, Zhaohan, Ge, G., Lu, H., Meng, Z., Wu, H., Yang, Q., Liang, J.

In Proceedings of ACM DIS '25 Companion    DOI   

Intro

Kinesthetic interaction systems on mobile augmented reality (MAR) platforms remain largely conceptual. To explore this design space, we present karP, an experiential prototype that serves as a proof of concept for integrating kinesthetic systems with MAR.


Design

karP's device features a manipulandum-exoskeleton design that supports kinesthetic input and output for striking gestures with handheld items. Leveraging the native capabilities of MAR, the smartphone functions simultaneously as the visual-spatial interface and spatial tracker, coordinating with the kinesthetic device to control the avatar's physical interaction in virtual environments.

karP's kinesthetic device is designed to be worn on the forearm in an exoskeleton-like configuration, allowing reaction forces during kinesthetic feedback to be supported directly by the user's body. The core manipulandum employs a two-joint spherical mechanism, whose mechanical structure is responsible both for capturing user input and delivering impulse haptic feedback. We specifically aligned the two joints with the user's wrist axes, which makes the two DoF independent. This alignment allows us to directly define the workspace based on the human wrist's natural range of motion and to compute impulse torques with respect to the wrist.

For actuation, we implemented the classic capstan structure, which amplifies the relatively weak torque output from compact brushless DC motors while ensuring smooth transmission during manipulation. The structural components are fabricated via 3D printing, assembled with screws, and connected using metal cables. All the components used are considered basic and highly accessible electromechanical parts, consistent with our vision of building an accessible and reproducible experiential prototype.

We developed karP's AR application for iOS to run on iPhones, leveraging SceneKit for scene construction, physics simulation, and kinesthetic feedback computation, and ARKit for device camera tracking, environmental understanding, and spatial frame stabilization.
Inspired by the interaction mechanisms found in XR games, we designed a Ball Playground for karP in delivering haptic I/O experinces. We introduce variation in the initial states of objects (static, dynamic), their physical properties (mass, radius), and the avatar's holding items, where all are unified by the core goal of engaging users through diverse and expressive physical interactions around the featured gesture.

karP demonstrated our preliminary exploration of integrating a complete and complex mechanical kinesthetic input / output system into a MAR platform. Our design achieved an initial level of playfulness while adapting to the inherent interaction style of mobile AR.

Tangible Compass: An Affordable and DIY-Friendly Handheld Kinesthetic Device for Haptic Accessible Navigation

Pan, Zhaohan, Ge, G., Jiang, Z., Meng, Z., Wang, L., Wu, H., Wang, L., Liang, J., Wen, H.

In Proceedings of ACM CHCHI '24    DOI   

Intro

While smartphone map navigation provides a convenient outdoor travel experience, its visual, audio, and vibration feedback mechanisms may fail to meet the accessibility needs of particular users or account for temporary obstacles. Various devices have been developed to overcome these limitations, but they are often accompanied by high prices or technological barriers, potentially hindering widespread adoption and research replication.
To address this, we aim to design a low-cost, open-source device providing additional sensory feedback to support accessible navigation. Therefore, we introduce Tangible Compass, a handheld haptic device as an add-on for smartphone navigation functions, providing force guidance in various accessible navigation scenarios.



Design

Tangible Compass is a compact one Degree of Freedom (1-DoF) handheld kinesthetic device transmitting forces in the left/right direction. It is designed and built from very fundamental 3D printing parts and electric components to feature low-cost and DIY-friendly accessibility, supporting distributed productio and research replication.

Tangible Compass's functionality is achieved through streaming simulated smartphone navigation data. Oirentation deviation on a navigating route will be rendered through the haptic gradient force to guide the user to the correct forward orientation. This is namely a "tangible compass" through haptics through intuitive resistence push.



Interactivity

We demonstrate more applications and user interactions of Tangible Compass that is considered also significant in accessibile navigation context. These scenarios utilizes the same Tangible Compass hardware setup but employs different sensor inputs and rendering algorithms.

Tangible Compass demonstrated an open-source and accessbile interdisciplinary engineering project on solving real world problems. We envision not only Tangible Compass but also kinesthetic haptic devices, in general, becoming more miniaturized and ergonomic, seamlessly blending into users' daily lives. We will explore integrating these devices into more compact forms, such as wristbands or standard smartphones, to investigate the possibilities of more types of sensory feedback in everyday portable devices.
